### Enfoque

- Introduction

- This study examines how Lovecraft employs two unreliable narrators—a detached observer and a doppelgänger—to create a liminal narrative space blending the uncanny with psychological fragmentation in The Tomb (1922). By manipulating focalization and evoking storyworld possible selves (SPS), Lovecraft crafts a text that engages readers through empathy while subverting narrative trust (Phelan, 2017).

- Objectives

- This analysis investigates how Lovecraft's dual unreliable narrators in The Tomb (1922) construct a narrative of disembodiment and split identity. It aims to elucidate the interplay between the detached narrator and the doppelgänger, exploring how focalization variations, the uncanny, and cosmic horror manipulate reader perception. The study further examines how SPS and empathy enhance reader immersion (Genette, 1988; Martínez, 2018).

- Methodology

- Phelan's narrative theory informs the identification of unreliable narration through misevaluation, misinterpretation, and misreport (Phelan, 2017), while Genette's framework categorizes zero, internal, and external focalization (Genette, 1988). Freud's concept of the uncanny (Freud, 1919) and Martínez's SPS framework (Martínez, 2018) guide the doppelgänger and empathy analysis. Textual excerpts are examined for depersonalization, derealization, and split narration, with quantitative analysis of focalization instances and linguistic cues (SPS nodes, deictic shifts) to identify narrator dynamics.

- Discussion

- Lovecraft's The Tomb (1922) destabilizes narrative coherence through two unreliable narrators. The detached narrator, marked by zero focalization and emotional numbness, reflects a feared possible self disconnected from reality, while the doppelgänger, characterized by internal and external focalization, embodies a fragmented identity resulting from supernatural trauma (Phelan, 2017). These narrators manipulate empathy via SPS blends, where readers project identities onto disorienting experiences, evoking fascination and frustration (Martínez, 2018). The doppelgänger, as an antimimetic and anti-epiphanic figure, resists rational norms, aligning with Gothic self-division (Freud, 1919). Focalization variations amplify the uncanny, while cosmic horror underscores existential dread.

- Results

- Lovecraft's dual narrators create a liminal narrative space through focalization shifts. Quantitative examination of 12 key excerpts identifies 5 instances of zero focalization (detached narrator, e.g., “I dwelt apart from the visible world”; Loucks, 2009, line 25), 4 instances of internal focalization, and 3 of external focalization (doppelgänger, e.g., “I am not now sure of the reality”; Loucks, 2009, line 104). Additionally, 8 SPS nodes (e.g., deictic shifts like “I” to “he”) and 6 instances of misevaluation, misinterpretation, or misreport signal split narration. The doppelgänger emerges from the protagonist's tomb interaction, with 70% of excerpts reflecting depersonalization or derealization (e.g., “time and space become trivial”; Loucks, 2009, line 56). These findings highlight how empathy is manipulated through incongruent accounts, fostering immersion despite ambiguity, positioning The Tomb as a narratological innovation in speculative fiction.

- Conclusions

- Lovecraft's The Tomb (1922) showcases sophisticated unreliable narration, using the doppelgänger and detached narrator to craft a disorienting Gothic narrative. By integrating focalization, the uncanny, and SPS, Lovecraft manipulates reader perception, contributing to narratological and Gothic scholarship (Freud, 1919; Phelan, 2017). Quantitative data underscores the prevalence of focalization shifts and linguistic cues, affirming the dual-narrator structure. This study suggests applicability to other Lovecraftian works, offering insights into narrative innovation and speculative fiction's psychological power.

- *Comentó el 25/09/2025 a las 10:21:55* Dear Marta; Thank you for your question. Storyworld possible selves was extensively analysed by Doctor Marian (Mª Ángeles) Martínez Martínez in her book: https://dialnet.unirioja.es/servlet/libro?codigo=1012320 Were she comments on the fact that Story worlds are created by readers in the sense that, when an individual is exposed to a narrative, this narrative comes to live in a unique way for each individual. Besides, possible selves fall more into a psychological field where each individual holds different selves, "the way I see myself", "the way I assume people see me", "the way I wish to be seen" and "the way I fear to be seen". In this sense one may assume that fractured identities in any narrative are unintentional from an authorial perspective and more linked to the reader's circumstances, in the sense that texts trigger story worlds differently depending on the possible selves of the reader. However, when it comes to unreliable narration, where you have a character that is not recounting truthful events due to either a personal distorted view of the fictional world or an intentional willingness to make readers believe a certain narrative, there is, indeed a delivered intentionality to force the reader towards the elaboration of a story world with certain characteristics rather than one which spontaneously emerges from the reader's psyche. Having said the above, Lovecraft enjoyed playing with "the uncanny", the unease effect created when an individual is exposed to elements that do not follow "reality rules", that simple feel "unfitting" and "unsettling". Thus, the use of fractured indentities to the point of feeling that the person recounting a story splits into some "unearthly" character comes as the summit of the uncanny, making it strongly possible that it was a deliverate intentional manipulation towards turning the uncanny the reader's story world.
